LLM and Reasoning

December 2024

First, let me clarify that I’m not dismissing the progress made by methods like Chain of Thought (CoT), Branch of Thought (BoT), Test-Time Compute, or entropy-based techniques. These approaches are undoubtedly steps in the right direction. In fact, I believe that in the coming years, models will become so advanced, and these methods of "System 2" thinking so refined, that they might truly feel like AGI.

However, philosophically, I still don’t think these systems will ever achieve what I’d call “true” reasoning. Here’s why:

At their core, Large Language Models (LLMs) are exactly that: language models. Their knowledge is entirely derived from the information contained within the language data they are trained on. Humanity has created vast amounts of knowledge. Through spoken, written, and digitized language, we’ve expressed our values, beliefs, ideas, reactions to situations, and so much more. LLMs learn from this linguistic representation of human thought. But everything they “know” is rooted in what we’ve explicitly or implicitly conveyed through language.

Now, even if we assume an LLM has ingested sufficient knowledge of the world through these linguistic patterns, it could likely solve a wide range of problems, from simple to highly complex. After all, if you have comprehensive knowledge and advanced System 2 reasoning capabilities, you can theoretically solve almost any problem. And I do believe future models will reach that point.

But here’s the key distinction: for humans, language is not the source of knowledge; it’s a container for it. We generate ideas, thoughts, and theories abstractly in our minds. Language is merely the tool we use to communicate and share those thoughts. Importantly, not everything we think is captured in language. Our minds are in a constant state of idea generation, much of which remains unspoken or unwritten.

In contrast, LLMs are limited to the ideas and knowledge we’ve chosen to encode in language. They can’t independently generate new, original ideas to expand their own knowledge base. Their "knowledge" grows only as we humans contribute to it.

In this sense, the knowledge base of an LLM is finite, albeit extraordinarily large, whereas human knowledge generation is boundless and not entirely tied to language. LLMs learn patterns and ideas expressed through language, a complex and rich tool we’ve developed. But this doesn’t mean LLMs are the originators of those ideas or capable of generating novel concepts that surpass the boundaries of their training data.
