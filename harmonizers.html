Harmonizers: A Theory

January 2025

I believe our brains are natural "harmonizers," meaning that when we acquire knowledge about a subject from multiple dimensions, our brains instinctively strive for the "optimal balance" of those dimensions. This assumes, of course, that we don't hold strong personal biases for or against certain aspects of the knowledge.

For example, consider a painter who learns about the science of colors. If this painter has a personal preference for yellow and a dislike for blue, then when creating a painting (depending on its context), their brain will naturally aim for the "optimal balance" of colors required. However, these personal biases will likely skew that balance, either favoring yellow more or minimizing blue, regardless of what might be objectively ideal for the painting.

The degree to which your brain can achieve this balance depends on how well you understand a concept. The deeper your understanding, the closer your brain gets to producing an unbiased and balanced perspective. Conversely, if you develop biases during the learning process, those biases will inevitably be reflected in the conclusions or outputs your brain produces.

I believe this concept applies universally. For instance, if a society only learns about its own culture, history, and values, essentially gaining knowledge from a single perspective, it will naturally be biased toward its own domain. If individuals within that society also have personal biases favoring their own culture, the "optimal balance" their brains produce will heavily favor their own values, and their understanding of society as a whole will remain limited. Furthermore, if this society learns about other cultures only through a negative lens, that bias against other domains will also distort the balance their brains create regarding how societies should coexist.

This process is recursive and never-ending. We can never completely eliminate personal biases because the knowledge we acquire is often influenced by the biases of those who produce it. These biases can propagate through our understanding. Therefore, the goal is not to lose your biases or your preferences but to approach a specific domain from as many dimensions as possible. This way, the "optimal balance" your brain creates will not be so heavily skewed that it loses the inherent beauty and richness of knowledge.
